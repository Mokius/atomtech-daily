### 12. 🔬 Physicists Observe Electrons Flowing Like Frictionless Liquid in Graphene

**Category:** `🔬 Science` | **Impact:** 🟡 Medium | **Source:** [Wikipedia — 2026 in science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_in_science)

**Summary**
A team of physicists reported on April 15 that electrons in graphene were observed flowing in a nearly frictionless "viscous" liquid state, in apparent violation of a foundational principle in condensed matter physics — the Wiedemann-Franz law. This phenomenon, called hydrodynamic electron flow, had been theoretically predicted but not cleanly observed at this level until now. The research was conducted using cryogenic graphene samples with extreme purity.

**Why It Matters**
The Wiedemann-Franz law describes the relationship between how well a material conducts heat versus electricity — it's been a cornerstone of materials physics for over 160 years. Observing a clear violation opens new theoretical territory and has practical implications for ultra-low-resistance electronics and quantum computing components.

**What Is This?**
Normally, electrons in a metal move somewhat chaotically, bumping into each other and losing energy as heat. In this experiment, electrons in graphene behaved like a smooth, coordinated fluid — losing almost no energy in the process. This could eventually lead to electronics that waste far less power.

**Looking Ahead**
This discovery could guide the development of next-generation low-power chips and superconductor-adjacent materials. Materials science groups will attempt to replicate this in other 2D materials.

**📱 Social Angle:** *Physicists just watched electrons flow like water — and it broke a 160-year-old law of physics.*
